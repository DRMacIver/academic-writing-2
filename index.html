writing style(s)

practical style

[In Practical style] the reader's need, not the writer's desire to articulate something, initiates the writing. The writer's job is to serve the reader's immediate need by delivering timely materials. [...] Since the reader is engaged in solving a problem, the reading is not an end in itself, it is instrumental to some other end.

Thomas and Turner, Clear and Simple as the Truth

point

rules of point

  1. decide what your point is.
  2. say it.
  3. don't say things that are not your point.

improving your point

  1. write your point explicitly in the text.
  2. delete text that isn't relevant to it.

flow

rules of flow

  1. introduce things before you use them.
  2. connect things to what came before.
  3. keep related things near to each other.

voice

rules of voice

  1. write as you want to be read.
  2. find a consistent tone.
  3. find a pace that guides the reader.

improving your voice

  1. decide on your tone up front.
  2. read what you say out loud.
    1. listen to the tone
    2. insert spaces where you pause naturally

improving your voice

This sentence has five words. Here are five more words. Five-word sentences are fine. But several together become monotonous. Listen to what is happening. The writing is getting boring. The sound of it drones. It's like a stuck record. The ear demands some variety. Now listen. I vary the sentence length, and I create music. Music. The writing sings. It has a pleasant rhythm, a lilt, a harmony. I use short sentences. And I use sentences of medium length. And sometimes when I am certain the reader is rested, I will engage him with a sentence of considerbale length, a sentence that burns with energy and builds with all the impetus of a crescendo, the roll of drums, the crash of the cymbals-sounds that say listen to this, it is important.

So write with a combination of short, medium, and long sentences. Create a sound that pleases the reader's ear. Don't just write words. Write music.

— Provost, 100 ways to improve your writing.

workflow

rules of workflow

  1. you should have one
  2. explicit is better than implicit
  3. design your workflow to help improve you writing

improving your workflow

  1. write it down and iterate on it
  2. everyone needs different things
  3. if it helps you it's a good idea

some of my good ideas

  • spoken drafts.
  • write one to throw away.
  • go for walks (without phone).
  • write a first draft in one sitting.
  • edit printed versions.
  • checklists for important features.

writing assignment checklist

  • make your point clearly in the first paragraph.
  • write more than you need then edit it down.
  • write a list of everything you need to explain.
  • read your essay out loud and edit for voice.
  • use a concept list (see last lecture).
  • use a user persona (see last lecture).
